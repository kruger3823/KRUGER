Through the damp mist, she stood smoking a cigarette, completely at ease. I tried to be subtle, but my eyes lingered a beat too long on the glowing ember in her hand.
Before I could look away, she caught my gaze. Time seemed to slow down. Instead of turning away, her expression softened, and she extended her hand toward me, offering the pack.
"Do you want a smoke?" she asked, her voice cutting through the steady drum of the rain.
Surprise flashed through me, a sudden warmth sparking in my chest. For a second, I could only stare, completely caught off guard by the unexpected kindness of a colleague whom I hadn't even talked.  The night started at my new company’s dinner party. Since I was planning to have a few drinks and let loose, I left my keys at home and took a cab to the venue. But the moment I walked in, the vibe felt completely off. I had just joined the office a few days ago, so I was the ultimate outsider. Everyone else already had their own tight groups, inside jokes, and shared history, which left me standing awkwardly on the sidelines trying to make forced small talk with people whose names I barely knew. It quickly became overwhelming and uncomfortable, so I decided to slip out early.
But the moment I stepped outside, my bad night got worse. A massive, heavy downpour had started, completely flooding the streets. I stood under the awning, desperately refreshing my phone, trying to book a ride home. Because the weather was so terrible, the apps were entirely jammed. Screen after screen timed out, and every single driver on the road rejected my ride requests, leaving me completely stranded in the middle of the storm.
  The rain finally started to calm down, slowing from a furious downpour into a gentle, steady drizzle. As the smoke from that shared cigarette drifted into the cool air, the tension from the night began to melt away.
Curious, I looked at her and asked why she had left the party so early. She shrugged, offering a faint, knowing smile. "The vibe just wasn't set for me," she replied, mirroring exactly how I had felt inside that crowded room.
It was a funny realization. We both laughed when we discovered that, despite having eaten enough food at the party to last us the next two days, we couldn't help but complain about how terrible it actually tasted. Standing there under the narrow tin roof, with the cigarette slowly burning down between us, our shared misery turned into easy conversation. In the middle of a stranded night, we had somehow found our own right vibe
 The easy conversation came to a sudden halt when a car pulled up through the drizzling rain. It was her husband, coming to pick her up. We said our goodbyes and parted ways, but as I watched her car disappear into the wet night, a strange, lingering feeling settled in my chest. She had something that I desperately wanted—that effortless comfort, that perfect, easy vibe.
With that thought heavy on my mind, I finally managed to get back to my flat.
I walked through the door, soaked and exhausted, to find a completely different world. There was my wife, my sweet heart, curled up lazily on the bed with her kittens. Seeing her there, a sudden wave of gratitude washed over me. I walked straight over and wrapped my arms around her, holding her tight.
Because I was still completely drenched from the storm, the sudden hug immediately made her uncomfortable in the funniest way. She squealed, slipping out of my wet embrace with a laugh, and ran to the closet.
She marched back over, handed me a dry towel, and gave me a playful look.
"Go have a bath and come back," she said, smiling. "Let's watch that episode 4."
 The next morning,  as I walked in, there she was—sitting quietly at a desk across the floor, working in a completely different team.
I took a breath, walked towards her, and greeted her. We chatted politely for a moment, asking about how i finally managed to get home in the storm. The conversation was pleasant enough, but the effortless, late-night tea-stall vibe was gone, replaced by corporate formality.
Then, out of the blue, the words slipped out before I could stop them.
"Hey, call me the next time you're going out for a smoke," I said.
Instantly, the air shifted. A thick, heavy tension settled between us, and a cloud of pure awkwardness seemed to hang right over our heads. The casual comfort from the night before vanished. She didn't smile or say yes; she just gave a slow, polite nod.
In that single, quiet moment, the message was loud and clear. I understood right then that she wasn't interested. The magic belonged strictly to the rainy night, and today, we were just colleagues again.  For the rest of the day, I made a conscious effort to blend in. I chatted with my own team members, grabbed coffee with a few people, and did my best to navigate the usual office small talk. I took my usual smoke breaks downstairs—this time alone, staring out at the dry pavement, trying to shake off the embarrassment. Coping with the new environment wasn't seamless, but as I forced myself to laugh at jokes and listen to team banter, the sting of the morning started to fade. I was just the new guy trying to find his footing, one ordinary day at a time.
 Then came a day when work piled up heavily. By the time I finally closed my laptop, the office was mostly deserted, the bright fluorescent lights humming over empty desks. Exhausted and needing a final reset before heading home, I walked over to the pantry.
There she was.
She was standing by the counter, completely quiet, just like the night we first met. I stood frozen for a split second, holding an unlit cigarette in one hand. I had just come in to grab a quick coffee before heading down to the designated smoking area, which had become my usual, solitary routine.
The silence of the late-night office stretched between us, carrying a faint echo of the rain from weeks ago.
 The sudden quiet of the pantry was heavy, but this time, I didn't let the awkwardness take over. I broke the silence casually with a simple, "Hi."
I walked past her toward the coffee machine and offered a small, easy smile. As I pressed the button, the machine came to life, filling the quiet room with the comforting, rhythmic sounds of bubbly boiling and a soothing, steady drip.
While the coffee was brewing, I looked over and asked a safe, ordinary question: "How's work going?"
That simple question was all it took. The wall she had put up at her desk weeks ago completely vanished. She broke her silence, the words pouring out of her as she talked a lot about her current project and the immense pressure her team was under.
As we talked, our vents and complaints naturally converged on a single, shared target: our Product Manager. We realised we both worked under the exact same person—the infamous PM who was constantly bringing in sudden, chaotic requirements from clients and dumping them on the teams with unrealistic deadlines.
Standing there by the humming coffee machine, our shared frustration turned the remaining office tension into instant, relatable laughter.
 Then, out of the blue, she looked at the cigarette in my hand and asked, "Can we go smoke?"
The vibe instantly shifted. It was too perfect.
As we walked down to the smoking area, the conversation caught fire. It turned out she was a total cinephile, and we shared a deep, burning interest in the exact same genres. For the first time in years, I felt a spark ignite inside me—something I thought had died a long time ago.
My wife is North Indian, so our life at home is lived in a blend of Hindi and English. While I love her, our cultural worlds are completely different. We watch totally different kinds of movies. She doesn’t know the regional films I grew up with, the epic native language dialogues, or the nostalgic pop-culture references that shaped who I am.
But standing there, exchanging lines and laughing over obscure movie scenes with this girl, it felt like finding a missing piece of myself. Under the dim lights of the smoking zone, trading dialogue after dialogue, the language barrier of my daily life vanished, replaced by an intoxicating, shared frequency.
 I offered her a ride to the nearest bus station. She paused for a second, then smiled and accepted.
That short, ten-minute drive flew by too fast. We didn't stop talking about movies the entire way.
And just like that, what started as a one-time favour quickly became our daily routine. Every evening, as the office began to empty, we would sync up our schedules. We’d grab that final coffee, head down for a smoke, and then walk together to my car.
  Over the next three months, our short evening drives evolved into a sanctuary of deep, unfiltered conversations. The safe barrier of casual movie banter slowly melted away, exposing the raw, quiet vulnerabilities we both carried beneath our professional exteriors.
We began discussing the profound things we felt were missing in our respective marriages.
I used to be an incredibly energetic guy—the kind of person who loved spontaneous adventures, exploring new places, and diving headfirst into life. But over the years, that spark had dimmed because I no longer had a partner who wanted to join me in those pursuits. At home, life had settled into a comfortable, quiet routine, leaving that restless energy inside me completely unfulfilled.
She, on the other hand, was a charismatic, brilliant woman trapped in a life she hadn’t entirely chosen. Beneath her sharp corporate attire was a lean, beautiful lady who dreamed of leaving the tech world behind to start her own boutique. But reality had chained her to her desk. The massive EMIs they had taken on to buy a house in a posh neighborhood had buried her alive in this office, forcing her to trade her creative passions for financial survival.
As we talked, I realized her entire youth had been a series of constraints. She hadn't been able to enjoy her twenties because she had been married off far too quickly. Even before that, romance had been entirely out of reach; her protective older brother was a senior in both her school and college, a looming figure who terrified every boy who ever thought about approaching her. She had skipped the thrilling, messy phases of young love and self-discovery entirely. Now, she was a woman longing to break free, desperate to experience something new and entirely her own.  "Go to the pubs where you never danced," I told her, my voice low in the quiet confines of the car. "Go to the concerts where you never sang. Wear something completely uncomfortable that you would normally never dare to wear."
She looked at me, her eyes wide under the dim streetlights outside the bus station, listening as I urged her to reclaim the stolen pieces of her youth.
"Break a rule," I continued, the intensity of the three months we had spent sharing our secrets rushing to the surface. "Do something just for yourself, without thinking about the house, the EMIs, or the expectations. Just go out there and live the years you missed."
 She realized that true masculinity was never about a body type, a towering stance, or an authoritative voice. It was always about this—the capacity for deep empathy toward a woman. It was the ability to truly see her, to listen to her silent suffocations, and to care about her happiness without needing to possess or control her.
